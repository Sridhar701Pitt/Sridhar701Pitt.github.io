# The Craft of Research (Booth et al.) – Chapter 2

Chapter 1 was great and taught me about the uses of research, public and
private. Now we are onto Chapter 2. Here we go…..

## Vocab for this chapter

1.  *Dogmatic* – To be dogmatic is to follow a set of rules no matter
    what. The rules might be religious, philosophical, or made-up, but
    dogmatic people would never waver in their beliefs so don’t event
    think of trying to change their minds.
    
    1.  If as we read we think, “Well, Abrams acknowledges Stanik’s
        evidence, but he is *dogmatic* in criticizing it and ignores
        obvious counterexamples,” Abrams can’t change what we read next
        to recover from our judgement.

2.  *Caricatures (v)* – A satirical, exaggerated portrayal of a person,
    thing, etc.
    
    1.  You’d better not caricature your father by walking bent over and
        speaking in his high voice. If he catches you, you’ll be in
        trouble.
    
    2.  Too many beginning researchers offer readers a relationship that
        *caricatures* a bad classroom.

3.  *Demean* – To demean is to degrade or put down a person or thing.
    
    1.  Do that and you turn your project into a pointless drill that
        *demeans* both you and your teacher.

4.  *Scoff* – To laugh at someone with scorn is to scoff at them.
    
    1.  Academic researchers are often *scoffed* at for studying
        esoteric topics that matter to no one but themselves.

5.  *Esoteric* – I you haven’t been brought into the inner circle of
    those with special knowledge, esoteric things will remain a mystery
    to you.
    
    1.  Academic researchers are often scoffed at for studying
        *esoteric* topics that matter to no one but themselves.

## Notes

1.  You must present yourself as interested in, even enthusiastic about,
    wanting to share something new, because the interest you show in
    your work roughly predicts the interest your reader will take in it.

2.  In this case, the old advice to “consider your audience” means that
    you must report your research in a way that motivates your readers
    to play the role you have imagined for them.

3.  Never mind weather your style is graceful (though I will admire your
    work more if it is); don’t bother me with amusing anecdotes about
    your Great-Uncle Otto (though I like hearing them if they help me
    understand your ideas better); ignore whether what you know will
    make me rich (though I would be happy if it did). Just tell me
    something I don’t know so that I can better understand our common
    interest.
